What determines a country's well-being?

This page displays information on European countries from either the Happy Planet Index or the Quality of Life Index.

You can choose either of the datasets using the dropdown button.

If you click on one of the countries of the map, the barchart will display both ranks for that country.

Below the visualizations you can find a short description of the data.

Description of data

An age-old psychological phenomenon is the human drive to compare ourselves to others (Festinger, 1954).
On top of this, people have the need to belong to groups. Therefore, people categorize themselves into several ingroups, and often compare themselves against an outgroup.
One often made ingroup is that of one's country (Tajfel, 1974). Thus, we often want to compare how our country is doing against others.

In addition, many people are interested to see how well countries are doing, and how happy a country's population is.
Several methods of analysis have been used to investigate this and to create an "index" that indicates a countries well-being.
The Happy Planet Index and the Quality of Life index are two such methods.

The Happy Planet Index aims to indicate how well countries are doing at achieving long, sustainable and happy lives.
To measure this, they analyze a country's well-being, life expectancy, inequality between the population (in terms of lifespan and happiness), and ecological footprint.

The Quality of Life Index, on the other hand, is an estimation of overall quality of life.
It takes into account health care, safety, purchasing power, pollution, house price to income ratio, cost of living, traffic commute time, and climate.

Apparently, both indices have different measurements to determine a country's wellbeing.
Of course, the question is: how well are these two methods at determining how well a country is doing? How do these two indices relate and compare to each other?

To investigate this issue, this visualization shows the Happy Planet Index and Quality of Life Index for 30 European countries.
The left visualization shows a map with the rank for each country. The right visualization shows a bar chart with each country's rank.
By using the drop-down button, you can switch between the two datasets. Immediately, it becomes apparent that these scores do not relate to each other that much.
When switching between datasets, you see that both the colours on the map as well as the bars change per country.

To zoom in on these differences, it is possible to click on a specific country on the map. The bar chart on the right will then visualize both the HPI rank and the QOL rank.
Some countries seem to have a similar HPI rank and QOL rank. For instance, the Netherlands ranks third on both measures.
However, there are also countries that score quite differently on both indices. Estonia, for example, scores 30th on the HPI Rank, but 8th on the QOL rank.
Another example is Spain, which scores 2nd on the HPI Rank, but 11th on the QOL Rank.

Thus, this visualization shows that "well-being" can be defined in different ways, and that these methods of determining well-being can result in different outcomes.
In addition, a bad score on one of these indices might not mean that a country is actually doing badly in terms of well-being, as this ultimately depends on the measurements.
This visualization thus nicely underscores the importance of not relying on a single experiment or dataset to investigate a certain issue or prove a certain hypothesis.
So remember, validation of measures, using multiple measurement methods and replication of results is necessary to make strong claims!

To conlude, this visualization leaves us with one remaining question that might never be answered:

What really determines a country's well-being?
